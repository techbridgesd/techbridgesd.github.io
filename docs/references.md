# References

Sources used for the statistics, findings, and claims on the **Research** page
and elsewhere on the site. Figures reflect the most recent reports available at
the time of writing — **please confirm against the latest published editions
before final submission**, as agencies update these numbers annually.

1. **Federal Bureau of Investigation, Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3).**
   *2023 Elder Fraud Report.* U.S. Department of Justice.
   <https://www.ic3.gov/AnnualReport/Reports/2023_IC3ElderFraudReport.pdf>
   — Used for: total fraud losses among adults 60+ (~$3.4 billion in 2023),
   average loss per older victim (~$33,915), complaint counts (101,000+), and the
   year-over-year loss trend (2021 ≈ $1.7B, 2022 ≈ $3.1B, 2023 ≈ $3.4B).

2. **Pew Research Center.** *Internet, Broadband & Tech Adoption Fact Sheets.*
   <https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/fact-sheet/internet-broadband/>
   — Used for: share of Americans 65+ who own a smartphone (~61%) and who use the
   internet (~75%).

3. **Federal Trade Commission (FTC).** *Protecting Older Consumers — Report to
   Congress.*
   <https://www.ftc.gov/reports/protecting-older-consumers-report-congress>
   — Used for: fraud reports and losses by age group, and context on how older
   adults are affected.

4. **National Council on Aging (NCOA).** *Top Financial Scams Targeting Older
   Adults.*
   <https://www.ncoa.org/article/top-5-financial-scams-targeting-older-adults>
   — Used for: common scam types and prevention guidance.

5. **AARP.** *Scams & Fraud resources, including AI-enabled fraud (voice cloning
   and deepfakes).*
   <https://www.aarp.org/money/scams-fraud/>
   — Used for: how scammers are using AI to make fraud more convincing.
